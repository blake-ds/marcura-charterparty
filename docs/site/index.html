Charter Party Document Parser

Extract every legal clause from a 39-page voyage charter party PDF into structured JSON. Strike-through edits are removed by reading the PDF's drawing rectangles directly — every word that ends up in clauses.json is traceable to a glyph on the source page.

How it works

One real clause through the whole pipeline. The PDF shows clause 6 with one phrase struck out — the parser tags struck glyphs from the PDF's own drawing rectangles, then assembles the surviving text into the deliverable JSON.

Source — PDF page 7, clause 6
Dues and other charges upon the vessel, including those assessed by reference to the quantity of cargo loaded or discharged, and any taxes on freight whatsoever shall be paid by Owners, and dues and other charges upon the cargo shall be paid by Charterers.
PyMuPDF rawdict + page.get_drawings() — 134k char bboxes, 794 thin rectangles
deterministic pipeline · five stages
Algorithm
  1. Load PDF PyMuPDF: per-glyph bboxes + page drawing rectangles
  2. Filter strikes tag a glyph "struck" if its bbox centre lies in a thin rect (h ≈ 0.42 pt)
  3. Detect sections match header text in top of page → SHELLVOY · Additional · Essar
  4. Parse clauses per section: find N. anchors, collect title and body chars in column
  5. Emit {id, title, text} JSON + a hand-styled browseable HTML
optional side-loop Azure LLM verifier — reads the emitted clauses as HTML-tagged input, returns strict JSON anomalies (truncated, merged, out-of-order). Advisory only; never rewrites text. A verifier outage cannot break a deterministic extraction.
clauses serialized

Why strike-through is the hard part

In this PDF strike-through is rendered as a thin filled rectangle drawn over the glyphs — not a PDF annotation, not a font flag. PyMuPDF exposes those rectangles through page.get_drawings(); a glyph counts as struck when its bbox centre lies inside one of them. Sound enough until a rectangle stops half-way through a word and leaves orphan glyphs visible.

PDF Owners shall be liable for any loss, damage or delay caused by the personal act or fault of the Master.
→
filtered text Owners shall be liable for any loss, damage or fault of the Master.

Three cleanup rules ride on top of the per-glyph mark, each one driven by a real failure mode in the corpus:

  • Position-aware whole-line vote. A line is dropped only when its first printable glyph is struck and the line is more than 50 % struck overall. This keeps lines whose visible content opens with real prose; it discards lines whose surviving glyphs are mid-sentence debris.
  • Word-level promotion. If any glyph inside a word is struck, the whole word goes. Catches the indemnity → "demnity" orphan-suffix pattern where a rectangle ends mid-word.
  • Sandwich rule. A clean-ish line tightly between two heavy-struck lines on the same page is also dropped — the surviving glyphs are debris from a rectangle that happened to end short of the line width.

Part II is three documents stitched together

Inside Part II (pages 6–39) the clause-numbering restarts three times. Each section uses a different layout, and the parser branches accordingly.

Numbering resets three times, so id="1" would appear three times under the brief's literal reading. The output prefixes each id with the section — shellvoy-1, additional-1, essar-1 — keeping the flat JSON list collision-free.

22 clauses are wholly replaced. Each has its title struck and its body either fully struck or reduced to mid-word debris by a partial rectangle (e.g. Canada Clause, Sidi Kerir Clause, Rotterdam Port Dues Clause). These are dropped on purpose. Every drop is documented page-by-page in eval/ground_truth.md.

The LLM verifier

The Azure-hosted LLM never writes clauses.json. It reads the deterministic parser output, formatted as <clause id="…"><title>…</title><text>…</text></clause>, and returns a strict-JSON list of anomalies (truncated, merged, out-of-order). The runner logs them as warnings and returns normally — a verifier outage must not break a deterministic extraction.

Is the LLM strictly necessary for this PDF? No — the deterministic parser already passes every eval gate against the hand-curated ground truth, and the LLM is deliberately scoped to a judge-not-author role. The principle: don't use an LLM where a deterministic algorithm suffices. Where it would earn its keep is generalisation — pointed at a different charter party (different boilerplate, different strike-through conventions, different column geometry), the hand-tuned rules above would need re-tuning, and a verifier that can reason about structure becomes genuinely load-bearing. Other directions worth exploring on a larger corpus (semantic clause-type tagging, automated ground-truth proposal, layout-class inference) are real ideas — but overkill for a single-document submission.

A handful of clauses surface advisory flags every run — shellvoy-4, additional-1, additional-11 for "text ends mid-sentence", and additional-1 sometimes for "two distinct subjects suggesting merged clauses". All known false positives: the PDF clause itself ends mid-sentence (struck tail) or contains a real discontinuity (struck region between two paragraphs of the same clause).
